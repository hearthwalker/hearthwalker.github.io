Trip #11 -- Lake Hudson SRA, MI

2026-04-19

Kid Trip #11 -- Lake Hudson, Michigan (for the second time). 1,682 miles, four kids, one weekend. The monthly trip cadence finally renewed with the end of winter.

The Drive

Left Albion at 5:30 Wednesday evening with a van full of gear, a cold Mountain Dew and a sky that looked like it was putting on a show just for the occasion. Pink thunderheads lit up the plains all the way across Nebraska and into Iowa -- the kind of sky that makes you remember why you drive instead of fly.

Fueled in Moscow, Iowa, grabbed a few hours of sleep at a rest area and rolled into Hicksville, Ohio at 4pm Thursday to pick up the crew. Four kids buckled in, chaos initiated, Michigan bound.

The Arrival

And April in Michigan did what April does. We pulled into the campsite under clear skies -- with ominous dark clouds stacking up to the west like something stalking us -- and had approximately ninety seconds to feel smug about our timing before the sky opened up and soaked us to the bone. Welcome to Lake Hudson.

By the time I got the 10x10 shelter popped open and the gear under cover, everyone was drenched, laughing, and already committed to the bit. This was going to be a mud trip. So be it.

The Campsite

Lake Hudson State Recreation Area sits just north of the Ohio border in Lenawee County -- a quiet, wooded dark sky preserve wrapped around a no-wake lake. It's the kind of place that doesn't advertise itself. You have to know to look.

We arrived to find our site thoroughly acquainted with three days of April rain. Three inches of mud, wall to wall. The kids didn't notice. Within twenty minutes they were filthy, delighted, and completely at home.

The Coleman Skylodge claimed its ground. Hot dogs went on the fire. Orange sodas cracked open all around. Trip #11 was officially underway.

The Mud

The ranger took pity on us the next morning after driving past and observing a slightly-feral group of children completely coated in mud, and let us swap to a slightly-less-muddy site. It stayed slightly-less-muddy for about an hour before five sets of feet turned it into its own mud pit. After that, there was no winning. The mud got into everything. Sleeping bags. Blankets. Tent floor. Inside every shoe and most of the socks. We washed feet at the tent door like a religious rite and lost the battle anyway.

By the end of the first day there was a sad little pile of mud-soaked shoes by the tent door and a quiet agreement between us all: barefoot the rest of the weekend, and so be it.

Xander embraced it harder than anyone. Slid sideways on a slick patch one afternoon and went full-body into the mud, laughing the whole way down and coming up looking like something that had crawled out of the riverbed to learn the ways of man. Rory took the exact opposite approach. She took one look at the mud and puddles between the tent and the restroom and declared that her mud-free feet were not going to participate, thank you. Fair enough. I carried her on my back, there and back again, more than once.

Dad love is a piggyback ride over a mud pit in April.

The Plano Box

Once the shelter was up that first night and the rain had mostly moved through, I set my late grandfather's tackle box on the picnic table and opened it. He carried it for decades and it's been waiting for the right moment since he passed. Four kids went quiet for about a second before absolute chaos erupted in the best possible way. Every lure examined. Every compartment raided. Questions fired faster than I could answer them.

By Sunday morning, all three boys had caught bluegill on his gear. Someone -- not naming names -- tried to eat one straight off the hook. That ancient, weathered Plano box has a new chapter now, written in small hands and bluegill slime under a Michigan sky. I think Grandpa would be happy with how it got written.

A Morning with Maire

My sister Maire stopped by one morning. I cooked eggs and bacon around the propane fire pit while the kids tore around the site burning off their usual morning energy, and she and I sat and caught up in that easy way siblings can when there's a fire going and nowhere else to be. It meant a lot that she made the drive.

The Cold Afternoon

One cold afternoon, when the wind cut through the trees and the fire just wasn't keeping up, we retreated into the tent, fired up the propane heater, and watched 'WondLa' on Apple TV (legit show, look it up). The kids demolished two bags of chips, a sleeve of cookies, and enough candy to stun a large raccoon. Dad rules apply on camp weekends. Camp calories don't count. Outside, the wind did its thing in the pines. Inside, four kids and their dad were warm, fed, and exactly where they were supposed to be.

The Drive Home

They chattered the whole way back to Ohio in the muddy minivan about next month's trip. Before we crossed the state line they were already planning what to pack, who was bringing what, and which campsite we should try to score.

That's the whole thing right there.

That's why I do the drive.

Trip #11. Many more to come. 🚐
